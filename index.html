A Documentary

Trial: October 13 – November 20, 2026

The Case Is Not the Story

On Mother's Day 2020, Suzanne Morphew went for a bike ride near her home in Salida, Colorado. She never came back.

Her husband Barry — a self-made millionaire, evangelical patriarch, and former Toronto Blue Jays draft pick — was arrested for her murder in May 2021. Charges were dropped just before trial in 2022. When her remains were found in a shallow grave in 2023 with animal tranquilizer in her bones, he was indicted again. He has been arrested for this crime twice.

The first collapsed case left wreckage behind it: the District Attorney was disbarred, the judge retired, FBI and CBI agents left their professions. Barry then sued every law enforcement officer involved for $15 million. The suit was dismissed — but its list of grievances is public record, and it is very wild. The system is trying to correct itself. Whether it succeeds is a thread of this story.

But the case is not the story.

The story is a poet driving alone to Alamosa — "The Coldest Town in Colorado" — to watch his childhood hero be destroyed, and to reckon with the mythology that made him.

Jason Morphew is Barry's first cousin and the only person in his family who believes Barry is guilty. The rest have closed ranks.

This film is about what it means to carry a name — and what it means to watch an institution try, a second time, to do what first it failed to do. If the justice system gets a second chance, does a family?

The genre is the foil. The subject is the self.

The Cultural Moment

This is not a story about a murder. It is a story about how a man's world — his faith, his family, his certainty about who is saved and who is not — can become a closed system that justifies everything.

Evangelicalism as control

Barry's theology — that this life is a 'mear grain of sand compared to eternity' — is not a fringe position. It is the operating system of tens of millions of Americans. This film asks what it does to a person — and to the people they have authority over.

MAGA and masculine authority

Barry was convicted of forging his missing wife's ballot for Trump — a felony, for which he received a suspended sentence and a small fine. That act — the husband as sole arbiter of the household — is the political made intimate and lethal.

The family as closed system

Every member of the Morphew family has closed ranks around Barry. Suzanne was attempting to escape — through her affair and her plans to leave the marriage. It worked, in the darkest possible way.

The cost of leaving

Jason's estrangement from his family is his attempt at escape. In this, he resembles Suzanne. The trial is the moment it becomes irreversible. That rupture is the emotional engine of the film.

Mother's Day, 2020

Suzanne Morphew was reported missing on Mother's Day, 2020 — a married mother of two, alone on a holiday she should not have been alone on. The detail is so strange it gave Nancy Grace her documentary its title: Mother's Day Murder. The country was in COVID lockdown. The film travels back to that specific American moment: isolation, fear, locked inside with someone you don't feel safe with.

The Protagonist

Jason Morphew

Yale BA. UCLA PhD in Renaissance Literature. Poet and singer-songwriter (Brassland, Ba Da Bing). Author of Eject City (2025) and dead boy (2018). Has written about the case for The Daily Beast. Has a press pass for the trial.

Grew up evangelical in Hot Springs, Arkansas — one of dozens of Morphew cousins — twelve siblings, each with children of their own. His father raised him to be an athlete; Jason made state all-stars in basketball, attended Bob Knight's Basketball Camp alongside Michigan's Fab Five. But when Barry was drafted out of high school by the Toronto Blue Jays, he assumed god-like status in the Morphew clan. Jason worshipped him.

He once marched against abortion rights at the Arkansas state capitol. Now takes his Jewish children to protest police brutality in Los Angeles.

The Positioning

Barry and Jason are first cousins — their fathers, Rodger and Joe, were two of the three youngest of twelve Morphew siblings. Barry grew up near Alexandria, Indiana — baseball legend, Blue Jays draft pick, evangelical patriarch. Jason grew up in Hot Springs, Arkansas — same evangelical God, different state.

Barry went deeper into the world that made them. Jason ran as far as he could — Yale, UCLA, Los Angeles, a Jewish family of his own making.

Every production company that has approached Jason has come with a formula. He has turned them all down. He is not looking for the highest bidder. He is looking for the right collaborator.

He once wanted to be Barry.

The Inheritance

Jason cannot determine whether he ever really left the world that made him. Alamosa is the only place he can find out.

The Name

Jason gave his two children the Morphew name before Suzanne disappeared. Because he's run as far away as he can from the cruel faith of his family, Jason's kids have never met another Morphew. He thought he could give them a name without a context, to generate an identity out of thin air.

The trial reveals what the name actually holds: a woman in a shallow grave, a theology of control, a family resolute against evidence.

The film is Jason looking directly at what he handed his children — and deciding, in real time, whether it can be redeemed or must simply be carried.

The Sedative in the Bones

Barry allegedly killed Suzanne with BAM, a highly regulated wildlife tranquilizer. Barry used it in Indiana for extracting semen from the bucks he kept in captivity there.

The autopsy found BAM in Suzanne's femur. Barry's indictment states that Barry possessed BAM illegally in Colorado and was the only person with BAM in that entire region of the country whose supply was unaccounted for.

What sedates a person into compliance? For Suzanne it was literal. For Jason it was evangelical certainty — the faith Barry used to justify everything, up to and including, Jason suspects, murder.

Jason woke up and ran. The trial is where he finds out whether he got it all out of his bones — or whether some of it is still in there, invisible, undetected, the way Suzanne's was.

The Institution on Trial

This is not a typical murder trial. It is the second prosecution of the same man for the same crime — and nearly everyone who touched the first one paid for it.

  • Second prosecution.Barry has been arrested for this crime twice. The first case was dismissed in April 2022 on the eve of trial.
  • A disbarred prosecutor.DA Linda Stanley lost her law license in 2024 over her conduct in this case and others. The Colorado Supreme Court upheld disbarment in 2025.
  • A judge who resigned.Ramsey Lama left the bench in his early forties, citing health reasons, three months after dismissing the original case.
  • Investigators off the case.CBI agent Joseph Cahill resigned under a Brady letter from his own director. FBI agent Jonathan Grusing departed the Bureau. Chaffee County Sheriff John Spezze stepped down.
  • A $15M civil suit, dismissed.The Tenth Circuit upheld dismissal of Barry's federal civil rights lawsuit on April 6, 2026 — but its 189-page complaint is public record, and spellbinding. It functions as a point-by-point dismantling of the original 2021 arrest affidavit.
  • The forensic science is itself contested.Of three labs consulted, only one detected BAM in Suzanne's femur. The defense will spend weeks attacking that lab's credibility.
  • A courtroom under siege.The trial court has restricted livestream access after true-crime channels violated prior orders.
  • A defense built on Suzanne's personal life.Barry's attorney has signaled the defense will surface accounts of Suzanne's affair and personal conduct to suggest alternative suspects. The same patriarchal logic — in death.

The system is trying to correct itself in real time. Jason is in the gallery while it does.

The Spy Pen

Suzanne suspected Barry of affairs and bought a voice-activated spy pen to catch him. She hid it in his truck and seems to have forgotten it was there. The FBI eventually recovered a recording of Suzanne herself — on a phone call, professing love to someone named "Jeff."

Jeff Libler was Barry and Suzanne's high school classmate. Suzanne and Jeff had been having a secret cross-country affair for two years. Suzanne's last proof of life is an erotic photo she sent to Jeff on the day before she was reported missing. According to the prosecution's theory, Suzanne was sexting Libler when Barry found her phone and killed her. When Libler learned she was missing, he destroyed all evidence of the affair and got a new phone. He did not contact law enforcement. He is married, with six young children. He has a rock-solid alibi. He told the FBI that when he heard Suzanne was missing, he had no doubt that Barry had learned of the affair and killed her.

This sequence — a spy pen, a secret affair, an erotic photograph, a man who said nothing — is the narrative heart of the prosecution's theory.

The Form

Jason's Vision

Jason's original pitch explicitly deconstructs the true crime format — naming and then swerving from each convention. He invokes REM's formal self-awareness: foregrounding the form to defamiliarize it.

The hook that isn't a hook. The investigation that turns inward. The resolution that doesn't resolve. The structure acknowledges what it's supposed to do — and refuses.

He is not performing grief. He will be candid and earnest. This film will feel like it costs something to make.

The Tradition It Belongs To

Stories We Tell (Polley, 2012) The filmmaker is inside the crime. Family memory as unreliable narrator. The investigation turns on the investigator.
Sugarcane (NoiseCat & Rezende, 2024) A filmmaker investigating his own family's institutional trauma. Inside the story, not outside it. Sundance Grand Jury Prize.
Procession (Greene, 2021) Survivors of clergy abuse reconstruct their past on camera. Religion, control, formally inventive, deeply personal.
Daughters (Love & Patton, 2024) Fathers in prison, daughters on the outside. Family fracture across an institutional divide. Sundance Grand Jury Prize.

The Audience

This film operates at the intersection of four audience segments that streamers are actively competing to serve — and a single acquisition serves all four.

True Crime Subscribers

Largest documentary segment on every major platform

The case has a built-in national audience — Dateline, 48 Hours, YouTube channels with hundreds of thousands of subscribers. But the true-crime audience has materially shaped this case: Stanley's disbarment was driven in part by her conduct on a true-crime YouTube show, and the trial court has had to restrict livestream access after channels violated the judge's orders.

The film's formal swerve from true-crime convention is not aesthetic preference. It is a response to what true-crime consumption has done to the thing being tried. That is a sharper proposition for buyers than "true crime done well."

The Evangelical Diaspora

~30M Americans have left evangelical Christianity

People who grew up in the same world Jason and Barry did — and got out, or tried to. This film speaks their exact language. The specific grammar of "witnessing," of eternity as excuse for the present, of husbands as household heads — this is not background noise for them. It is autobiography.

Prestige Documentary Audience

Festival-to-streaming: Sundance → HBO / Netflix / A24

The audience that drove Daughters, Sugarcane, and Procession to awards attention. Educated, 28–55, urban. They want films that use a particular story to say something true about America. This film has that engine. Jason's voice — a Yale-and-UCLA poet who once marched against abortion — is sui generis.

The Political Moment

MAGA, gender, power — and the 2026 midterm cycle

Barry cast his missing wife's ballot for Trump while under suspicion for her murder. That single act sits at the intersection of every live political argument in America: domestic control, masculine authority, Christian nationalism, election integrity. The trial lands in October 2026 — the midterm cycle. The timing is not incidental.

The Stakes

The Apostate asks whether American institutions — the church, the family, the courts, the press — can hold a man like Barry Morphew accountable. And whether the cousin who got out was ever really free of him.

Suzanne Morphew tried to leave the marriage and was killed. Linda Stanley tried to convict him and was disbarred. Iris Eytan tried to defend him and left criminal practice for legislative reform. Jason Morphew is going to Alamosa to find out what is still in his bones.

The trial is the test. The film is the witness.
