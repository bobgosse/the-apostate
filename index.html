THE APOSTATE

A Documentary

One man. One trial. The family he can't escape
and the name he gave his children.

"This life is a mear grain of sand compared to eternity."

— Barry Morphew, text to Suzanne
the day she disappeared

Trial: October 13 – November 20, 2026

The Case Is Not the Story

What we know: Barry Morphew was first arrested for Suzanne's murder in May 2021. Charges were dropped just before trial in April 2022. After Suzanne's remains were found in a shallow grave in 2023 — with animal tranquilizer in her bones — he was indicted again in June 2025. He has been arrested for this crime twice.

When the first case was dismissed, Barry sued every law enforcement officer involved for $15 million in restitution. The suit was thrown out — but its list of grievances is public record, and it is very wild.

The evidence is damning. The case is strong. The verdict is coming.

The story is a poet driving alone to a small Colorado city to watch his childhood hero be destroyed — and to reckon with the mythology that made him.

Jason Morphew is the only person in his family who believes Barry is guilty. The rest have closed ranks.

This film is about what it means to carry a name.

The genre is the foil. The subject is the self.

The Cultural Moment

This is not a story about a murder. It is a story about how a man's world — his faith, his family, his certainty about who is saved and who is not — can become a closed system that justifies everything.

Evangelicalism as control

Barry's theology — that this life is a 'mear grain of sand compared to eternity' — is not a fringe position. It is the operating system of tens of millions of Americans. This film asks what it does to a person — and to the people they have authority over.

MAGA and masculine authority

Barry cast his missing wife's ballot for Trump while under suspicion for her murder. That act — the husband as sole arbiter of the household — is the political made intimate and lethal.

The family as closed system

Every member of the Morphew family has closed ranks around Barry. Suzanne was the only other one who got out — through her affair and her plans to leave the marriage. It worked, in the darkest possible way.

The cost of leaving

Jason's estrangement from his evangelical family is decades in the making. The trial is the moment it becomes irreversible. That rupture is the emotional engine of the film.

The Protagonist

Jason Morphew

Yale BA. UCLA PhD in Renaissance Literature. Poet and singer-songwriter (Brassland, Ba Da Bing). Author of Eject City (2025) and dead boy (2018). Has written about the case for The Daily Beast. Has a press pass for the trial.

Grew up evangelical in Hot Springs, Arkansas — one of twelve Morphew cousins. His father raised him to be an athlete; Jason made state all-stars in basketball, attended Bob Knight's Basketball Camp alongside Michigan's Fab Five. But when Barry was drafted out of high school by the Toronto Blue Jays, he assumed god-like status in the Morphew clan. Jason worshipped him.

He once marched against abortion rights at the Arkansas state capitol. Now takes his Jewish children to protest police brutality in Los Angeles.

The Positioning

Barry and Jason are first cousins — their fathers, Rodger and Joe, were two of the three youngest of twelve Morphew siblings, close as brothers. Barry grew up near Alexandria, Indiana — baseball legend, Blue Jays draft pick, evangelical patriarch. Jason grew up in Hot Springs, Arkansas — evangelical too, same God, different state.

Barry went deeper into the world that made them. Jason ran as far as he could — Yale, UCLA, Los Angeles, a Jewish family of his own making.

Every production company that has approached Jason has come with a formula. He has turned them all down. He is not looking for the highest bidder. He is looking for the right collaborator.

He once wanted to be Barry.

The Inheritance

Jason cannot determine whether he ever really left the world that made him. Alamosa is the only place he can find out.

The Name

Jason gave his children the Morphew name before he knew what it contained. He couldn't give them the extended family — they've never met another Morphew. So he gave them the name alone, stripped of context, as an act of identity.

The trial reveals what the name actually holds: a woman in a shallow grave, a theology of control, a family sealed against evidence.

The film is Jason looking directly at what he handed his children — and deciding, in real time, whether it can be redeemed or must simply be carried.

The Sedative in the Bones

Barry allegedly killed Suzanne with BAM — a wildlife tranquilizer used to sedate deer. The autopsy found it in her femur.

What sedates a person into compliance? For Suzanne it was literal. For Jason it was evangelical certainty — the faith that Barry used to justify everything, up to and including, Jason suspects, murder.

Jason woke up and ran. The trial is where he finds out whether he got it all out of his bones — or whether some of it is still in there, invisible, undetected, the way Suzanne's was.

The Human Architecture

Jason Morphew

Protagonist

Yale-educated poet. The only Morphew who believes Barry is guilty. Worshipped Barry as a child. Going to Alamosa alone to watch his childhood hero be destroyed — and to find out whether he ever really left the world that made them both.

Barry Morphew

The Accused

Former Blue Jays draft pick. Self-made millionaire. Survivalist hunter. Devoted evangelical. After the first case was dismissed, he sued every law enforcement officer involved for $15 million. The suit was thrown out — but the document is public, and it is extraordinary.

Suzanne Morphew

The Victim

Warm, kind, a cancer survivor. Was having an affair. Had begun drinking wine. Was actively taking documented steps to leave the marriage. She got out — in the darkest possible way. Her daughters stand behind her accused killer.

The Family

Greek Chorus

A large Indiana evangelical clan — Barry's and Jason's fathers were two of twelve siblings — that has circled the wagons completely. Macy and Mallory Morphew attend every hearing at their father's side. Jason is persona non grata.

The Archival World

This case has been generating material for five years. The archival landscape is unusually rich.

  • Barry's Blue Jays draft coverage: newspaper clippings, sports photography, local TV
  • Morphew family photographs across two generations
  • Suzanne's active social media presence: posts, photos, video — a documented life
  • Jason's own materials: poems, song recordings, photographs, letters
  • Barry and Suzanne's texts, including Barry's final message to her

News & Court

  • Five years of national news footage: Dateline, 48 Hours, Good Morning America (×2), local Colorado TV
  • Barry's multiple on-camera TV interviews — processed grief, evolving narrative
  • The 130-page arrest affidavit: a documentary in itself
  • Court proceedings: six weeks of trial footage via credentialed press
  • Forensic photographs and evidence exhibits entering public record at trial
  • DA Linda Stanley's media appearances — a prosecution that destroyed its own prosecutor

Legal & Forensic

Trial journalists Reporters embedded for six weeks — including Denver journalist Carol McKinley, the primary reporter on the case — who provide the procedural scaffolding Jason's subjectivity doesn't supply.

The Form

Jason's Vision

Jason's pitch explicitly deconstructs the true crime format — naming and then swerving from each convention. He invokes REM's formal self-awareness: foregrounding the form to defamiliarize it.

The hook that isn't a hook. The investigation that turns inward. The resolution that doesn't resolve. The structure acknowledges what it's supposed to do — and refuses.

He is not performing grief. He will be candid and earnest. This film will feel like it costs something to make.

The Tradition It Belongs To

Stories We Tell (Polley, 2012) The filmmaker is inside the crime. Family memory as unreliable narrator. The investigation turns on the investigator.
Sugarcane (NoiseCat & Rezende, 2024) A filmmaker investigating his own family's institutional trauma. Inside the story, not outside it. Sundance Grand Jury Prize.
Procession (Greene, 2021) Survivors of clergy abuse reconstruct their past on camera. Religion, control, formally inventive, deeply personal.
Daughters (Love & Patton, 2024) Fathers in prison, daughters on the outside. Family fracture across an institutional divide. Sundance Grand Jury Prize.

The Director

This film needs a director who understands that Jason's consciousness IS the film — not a device for delivering the case.

Penny Lane

Hail Satan? · Escapes

Her films engage evangelical culture and American belief head-on. Formally playful, emotionally honest. Knows exactly what Jason is trying to do with the genre.

Heidi Ewing

Jesus Camp · I Carry You With Me

Has spent her career inside faith communities. Knows how belief becomes control. Deep empathy for subjects she's simultaneously interrogating.

Kitty Green

Casting JonBenét · The Assistant

Casting JonBenét refuses to deliver the true crime goods — it turns the genre inside out. She would understand this project's formal ambition immediately.

Alma Har'el

Bombay Beach · LoveTrue

Works in lyric documentary territory — personal, wandering, poetic. Would match Jason's sensibility as a poet and songwriter. Form over formula.

Theo Anthony

All Light Everywhere

Formally adventurous. Treats the act of filming as part of the subject. His films ask what it means to look at something — which is exactly what this film asks.

Jason has turned down everyone who came to him with a formula. He needs a collaborator who will build the form with him.

The Audience

This film operates at the intersection of four audience segments that streamers are actively competing to serve — and a single acquisition serves all four.

True Crime Subscribers

Largest documentary segment on every major platform

The case has a built-in national audience — Dateline, 48 Hours, YouTube channels with hundreds of thousands of subscribers. Those viewers will come for the case. The film holds them with something the formula never delivers: genuine interiority and a family member inside the house.

The Evangelical Diaspora

~30M Americans have left evangelical Christianity

People who grew up in the same world Jason and Barry did — and got out, or tried to. This film speaks their exact language. The specific grammar of "witnessing," of eternity as excuse for the present, of husbands as household heads — this is not background noise for them. It is autobiography.

Prestige Documentary Audience

Festival-to-streaming: Sundance → HBO / Netflix / A24

The audience that drove Daughters, Sugarcane, and Procession to awards attention. Educated, 28–55, urban. They want films that use a particular story to say something true about America. This film has that engine. Jason's voice — a Yale-and-UCLA poet who once marched against abortion — is sui generis.

The Political Moment

MAGA, gender, power — and the 2026 midterm cycle

Barry cast his missing wife's ballot for Trump while under suspicion for her murder. That single act sits at the intersection of every live political argument in America: domestic control, masculine authority, Christian nationalism, election integrity. The trial lands in October 2026 — the midterm cycle. The timing is not incidental.
